## The three-tier reality

Foreign cards are useful in China, but they are not the main payment layer for a budget DIY trip.

Use this order:

- Tier 1: mobile payment for about 90% of daily situations.
- Tier 2: cash as the fallback when phones, apps, or risk controls fail.
- Tier 3: physical foreign cards for specific internationalized places.

Start with [Alipay](/money/alipay/), add [WeChat Pay](/money/wechat-pay/), and keep [cash](/money/cash/) ready. Use this page to decide when direct card payment is worth trying.

## Before you fly checklist

- Turn on overseas card use.
- Bring two cards from different card networks.
- Tell your card issuer your travel plan.
- Confirm your PIN before leaving.

## Logo does not mean acceptance

A Visa or Mastercard sticker on the door does not guarantee your foreign-issued card will work.

The practical reason is simple: some merchant setups are built for China-issued cards, UnionPay, Alipay, or WeChat. A foreign-issued card can still be declined even when a familiar card-network logo is visible.

If a card fails, switch to [Alipay](/money/alipay/), [cash](/money/cash/), or the merchant's supported payment route instead of retrying the same failed POS path.

## Shanghai Metro card tap-to-pay

Shanghai Metro is the clearest direct-card exception in this brief.

As of 2025-06, foreign visitors can use physical contactless Visa, Mastercard, American Express, and JCB bank cards to tap through Shanghai Metro gates across the full 500+ station network. As of 2026-03, the service had recorded 1.6 million+ rides.

For broader travel days, keep this as a Shanghai-specific transport exception, not proof that your card will work everywhere else. For intercity travel planning, use the [China trains guide](/trains/).
