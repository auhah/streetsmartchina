## Short answer

You can travel mostly on mobile payment in China, but carry ¥500-1,000 as backup cash.

From 2026-02-01, offline merchants refusing cash is illegal under the PBOC/NDRC/NAFR joint rule announced on 2025-12-19. Enforcement is tightening, and that rule is your basis for treating cash as a real fallback.

Start with [Alipay](/money/alipay/) as the main setup, keep this cash plan as backup, and return to the [money hub](/money/) for the full payment stack. For the card you use at ATMs, keep the [foreign cards guide](/money/cards/) on your list. Add cash planning to the [China travel checklist](/china-travel-checklist/).

## When you will actually need cash

- Your phone battery is dead.
- Your payment account hits risk controls.
- A small stall or old local shop is easier to handle with cash.
- A temple donation box takes cash.
- You need an emergency fallback.

## Getting RMB from ATMs

Use ATMs as the verified cash route in this guide. Bank of China, ICBC, and CCB are the most stable banks for foreign cards.

| ATM issue | Verified guidance |
| --- | --- |
| Bank choice | BOC, ICBC, and CCB are the most stable for foreign cards. |
| Chinese-side ATM fee | About ¥20-30 per withdrawal. |
| Single withdrawal limit | ¥3,000 per transaction. |
| Daily withdrawal limit | About ¥10,000 per day. |
| DCC trap | Always choose RMB debit. |

## Airport exchange vs ATM

Airport exchange is separate from the ATM plan above. The verified ATM details are the bank choice, Chinese-side ATM fee, single withdrawal limit, daily withdrawal limit, and the DCC rule to choose RMB debit.

## Using cash

RMB notes run from ¥1 to ¥100. Keep smaller notes when you can because cash is your fallback for small stalls, old local shops, temples, and emergencies.

Counterfeit cash risk is lower in the mobile-payment era, but still treat old or suspicious notes carefully.
